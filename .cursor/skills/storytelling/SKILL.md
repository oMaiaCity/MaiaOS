# Storytelling Skill

Use this skill when planning or reviewing video content, short-form social clips, marketing narratives, or content strategy. The goal is **strategic, story-driven content** that earns attention through connection—not noise.

## Core Principle

**The loudest voice doesn't win. The clearest one does.**

Avoid "Sensory Overload Marketing": fast cuts, generic upbeat music, disconnected imagery, volume over substance. The brain craves connection and causality, not chaos.

---

## The Three Questions (Before Creating Content)

Replace default tactics with these strategic questions:

| Instead of asking… | Ask instead… |
|-------------------|--------------|
| "How do we stop the scroll?" | **What is the one feeling we must create in the first three seconds?** |
| "How long should the video be?" | **What is the one idea we need our audience to remember?** |
| "What do we want to say?" | **What do we want our audience to do after they've felt something?** |

**Shift:** Create moments that move people to act. A feeling bridges the gap between seeing and doing.

---

## Neuroscience Principles

1. **The brain craves narrative** — It locks onto structure: problem, insight, resolution. Random sequences trigger confusion, not engagement.

2. **Emotion is the gateway to memory** — Genuine human expression fires mirror neurons. Real faces, real stories, real emotion create trust. One authentic frame beats ten seconds of generic B-roll.

3. **Clarity is louder than volume** — Most videos are watched with sound off. A single powerful question or statement in text, speaking to what the audience is already thinking, cuts through more than loud music.

---

## What Works in Short-Form Video

1. **Start with a face, not a logo** — Human connection first. A genuine human expression registers as relevance.

2. **Ask the question they're already thinking** — Don't pitch in the first three seconds. Mirror their struggle: "Tired of being the best-kept secret?" That's not a pitch—it's recognition.

3. **Structure with story, not just tips** — Even in 30 seconds: beginning (problem), middle (insight), end (shift in perspective). The brain craves this structure.

4. **Design for sound-off viewing** — Text on screen must work *with* the visual story. Use subtitles to emphasise key emotional beats, not walls of text.

5. **Every frame must serve the story** — No filler. No generic transitions. If it doesn't advance the narrative or deepen emotion, cut it.

---

## Anti-Patterns (Avoid)

- Fast cuts for the sake of speed
- Generic upbeat music as emotional shortcut
- Stock footage that could be anyone, anywhere
- Telling what you do before showing you understand their problem
- Relying on voiceover/music when sound is off
- Frames that don't advance narrative or emotion
- Content that gets views but doesn't move to action

---

## Content Checklist

Before publishing short-form content, verify:

- [ ] First three seconds: Human face + one clear feeling?
- [ ] Hook: Mirrors audience's struggle or question?
- [ ] Structure: Beginning (problem) → Middle (insight) → End (perspective shift)?
- [ ] Sound-off: Key beats readable as text?
- [ ] Every frame: Serves story or emotion?
- [ ] CTA: What should they *do* after feeling something?

---

## Template: Short-Form Script (30–60 sec)

```
OPENING (0–3 sec): [Face + one feeling. Question they're already thinking.]
PROBLEM (3–15 sec): [Their struggle. Show understanding.]
INSIGHT (15–40 sec): [One idea to remember. Narrative, not tips.]
SHIFT (40–50 sec): [Perspective change. Emotional payoff.]
CTA (50–60 sec): [What to do after they've felt something.]
```
